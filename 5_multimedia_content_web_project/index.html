Overview

As we all know, Spanish is one of the most widely spoken languages in the world, either as a mother tongue or as a second language. With Spain as its "Mecca", this rather pluricentric language spreads further on up to the other side of the ocean, onto the vast lands of Central and South America.
Its history traces back to the Indo European family and runs through Latin. Although there are no clear-cut dates, standard Spanish is considered to have been made popular by Alfonso X, king of Castile and Leon (1252-1284). During his reign, Latin as the vernacular of certain administrative documents was replaced by more dialectal versions of Spanish. During the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, the Spanish Crown embarked itself on several expeditions overseas. The main spots worldwide that received the Spanish conquerors were the Canaries, America, the Mediterranean and the Balkans, and the Philippines.

Along with these people came the language to what later on came to be the Argentine Republic. Now all of this happened hundreds of years ago. But what about now? Right now the Spanish language looks somehow like this:

it's basically an SVO language
there are prepositions... many!
it's a NOUN-ADJECTIVE language, unless we want to get really poetical
it's a NOUN-GENITIVE language, and there's nothing poetry can do about it
it's strongly suffixing, I mean it

So basically Spanish speakers are all over the world, but here we will focus on one of the spots in South America: Argentina. More precisely, on the place where standard Argentine Spanish is nowadays considered to be spoken: Buenos Aires.

S | C | Z

As opposed to the Spanish spoken in Spain, in Argentina both "s", "c" and "z" are pronounced in the same way, namely [s]. Let's listen to this aspect in the words "caza", meaning "hunt.PRES.1.SG", and "casa", meaning "house".

ASPIRATED S

Every so often it happens that an [s] is cought in between an open vowel, such as [o] and a velar plosive, such as [k]. For Argentine speakers it is much more comfortable to produce an aspiration, and realize the sound as [h], rather than to pronounce an actual [s]. But don't take my words, better listen to the word "bosque", which means "forest".

B | V

Something that saves Spanish techers loads of corrections when pronounciation is at stake is that there's no diference between the letters "v" and "b". Listen to the words "vaca", meaning "cow" and "boca", meaning "mouth".
